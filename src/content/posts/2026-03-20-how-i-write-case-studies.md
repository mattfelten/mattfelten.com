title: How I Write Case Studies

I've been on both sides of the hiring table. I've put together case studies trying to land a role, and I've reviewed hundreds of portfolios trying to fill one. That second experience changed how I think about the first.

Most case studies are built to be thorough. They walk through every phase, every decision, every outcome. That instinct makes sense. You did a lot of work and you want to show it. But thoroughness optimizes for the wrong moment.

## The first reader

The first person to see your case study is usually a hiring manager or recruiter with a stack of applications. They're not settling in with coffee to study your process. They're scanning. Fast. Trying to figure out if you're worth a conversation.

I've been that person. You develop a rhythm. Open portfolio, scroll, get a vibe, move on. It's not ideal, but it's real. When you have forty applicants and a meeting in twenty minutes, that's how it goes.

## Not the interview

Here's the thing that reframed everything for me: the case study's job is to get you to the interview. Not to be the interview.

That distinction changes what you optimize for. Instead of completeness, you optimize for clarity, speed, and enough signal that someone wants to learn more. The deep dive happens in the conversation. The case study just has to earn that conversation.

## What I landed on

After a lot of iteration, I settled on a loose template. Not a rigid formula. More like a checklist I run through when I'm putting one together.

```markdown
# Project Title
A hook sentence or two. What was the problem, what did you do, what happened.
This is the most important part of the entire case study.

## Screens
Enough visuals to build excitement before someone reads a single word.
Types that tend to work well:
- Hero shot (the main thing)
- A before/after or key state change
- Detail shots that show craft
- Mobile or responsive views if relevant

## Context
Company, timeline, team size. One or two sentences max.

## Your Role
What you specifically did. Be precise about scope:
solo vs. team, IC vs. lead, which parts were yours.
If you collaborated closely with others, say how.

## The Design Problem
One core challenge. Not a feature list.
Show how you thought about it, not just what you made.

## Outcomes
Metrics if you have them. Qualitative results if you don't.
Omitting this section is worse than imperfect data.
Optional: one sentence on what you'd do differently.
```

I don't follow this exactly every time. Some projects need more context, some need less. But it covers the pieces that I've found actually matter when someone is scanning.

---

## The hook earns everything

The first two or three sentences of a case study do almost all the work. If those don't land, nothing else gets read. I've watched myself skip past portfolios with great work because the opening didn't give me a reason to keep going.

A good hook answers three questions fast: what was the situation, what did you do, and what changed. Not in detail. Just enough to create a thread someone wants to pull.

## Enough screens to build excitement

Before anyone reads a word, they're going to scroll through your images. This is your first impression, and it happens in seconds.

You don't need a specific number, but you need enough that someone viewing them gets excited about the work. Too few and there's nothing to react to. Too many and it becomes a wall.

I think about types of shots rather than a count. A hero shot of the main thing. A before/after or a key state change. A detail that shows craft. Maybe a mobile view. Each image should make someone want to know the story behind it.

## Be specific about your role

"Led design for the project" doesn't tell me much. "Sole designer on a four-person team, responsible for research, interaction design, and the component library" does.

Being specific helps a hiring manager understand your actual scope. Are you an IC who owns execution end-to-end? A lead who directed other designers? Someone who partnered closely with engineering on implementation? These are different profiles and they're looking for specific ones.

Collaboration signal matters here too. Saying you worked with research, or partnered with a PM to define scope, or paired with engineers on feasibility shows you operate on a team. That's not a footnote. For a lot of roles, it's a requirement.

## One design problem, not a feature list

The biggest mistake I see in case studies is treating them like a feature tour. Screen after screen with descriptions of what each one does. That shows output, not thinking.

Pick one core design challenge from the project. The gnarliest constraint, the most interesting tradeoff, the decision that shaped everything else. Then show how you worked through it. What options you considered, what you traded off, why you landed where you did.

This is where a hiring manager learns the most about how you actually work. Not from seeing the final screens, but from understanding the decisions behind them.

## Outcomes, even incomplete ones

I get why people skip this section. The project got deprioritized. The metrics aren't impressive. You left before launch. It feels safer to just not mention it.

But omitting outcomes is worse than imperfect data. When I'm reviewing a portfolio and there's no outcomes section, I don't assume the results were fine. I assume you either didn't measure or didn't want to share. Neither is great.

If you have metrics, use them. If you don't, qualitative results work. "Engineering shipped it in half the estimated time because the spec was tight." "Customer support tickets for this flow dropped after launch." "The team adopted this pattern across three other features." Even a single sentence about what you'd do differently shows reflection.

## Captions explain decisions, not screens

If someone can see what's on the screen, don't describe it to them. "This is the settings page" is wasted space. "We moved the most-changed settings to the top after watching users dig for them in testing" tells a story.

Every caption is a chance to show thinking. Use them to explain why, not what.

## The case study is a design artifact

Your presentation choices are being evaluated too. Typography, layout, hierarchy, how you sequence information. The case study itself is a design artifact, and hiring managers notice.

This doesn't mean it needs to be flashy. It means it should be intentional. Clear hierarchy. Good pacing. Images that are crisp and well-cropped. The same care you'd put into the work itself.

## The collection tells a story

If you're sending two or three case studies, think about what they show together. Do they demonstrate range? Different problem types, different scales, different industries? Or are they three variations of the same project?

The portfolio-level story matters. A hiring manager isn't just evaluating individual pieces. They're building a picture of what kind of designer you are and where you'd fit.

## Where it gets read

One more thing that changed how I format these: think about where they actually get viewed. On a phone between meetings. In an email forward from a recruiter. On a laptop with twelve other tabs open.

Long, image-heavy pages that require a desktop browser and focused attention are optimizing for a viewing context that rarely happens in the first round. Structure and clarity win over richness at this stage.

---

## Getting in the room

The full, thorough, detailed case study is what you bring to the interview. It's the version you walk someone through when you have their attention and thirty minutes to tell the story.

But before that happens, something shorter has to earn you the seat. That's what this is for.
