[Mission Cloud's](https://missioncloud.com/) Technical Account Managers spent up to five hours rebuilding the same slide deck for each customer, every month. I spent 14 months replacing it with Collaboration Loops, a feature inside Mission Control, Mission's web app for customers to manage their services and view insights about their AWS environments. Sole designer across the full arc, working with one PM and nine engineers, a ton of stakeholders, and a handful of customer interviews. Research through production React.

---

## The Problem

TAMs run recurring check-ins each month across 30+ customers each. Every meeting meant pulling data from multiple third-party tools and manually assembling a slide deck or Google Doc. Then they'd do it all again the next month.

Customers had no way to see what was discussed before. If a TAM transitioned a customer to a colleague, the history didn't follow. New hires on either side started from zero. The information and decisions from those meetings weren't tracked in a way anyone else at Mission, or on the customer's end, could get to.

The tools that existed treated these meetings as one-sided reporting events. What we needed was a space where both Mission's team and their customers could prepare, contribute, and look back together.

---

## The Design Challenge

This framing didn't come together all at once. But here's where we landed.

The obvious solution was a better template, a structured document TAMs could fill out instead of building slides. We deliberately didn't build that.

A document is static. It captures a moment, then goes stale. What we needed was something that stayed alive between meetings, pulling fresh data from multiple systems, preserving what was decided last time, and giving customers a reason to come back and actually contribute. Add topics to the agenda before a meeting. Approve recommendations themselves. Make the space theirs too, not just something they're invited to view.

That meant solving for several constraints at once.

I dug into the two-sided nature of the problem.

## Finding patterns

I collected 15-20 real meeting artifacts from TAMs and CSMs. The actual slide decks, Google Docs, and handwritten notes they were already using. Then I affinity mapped them, pulling apart the recurring building blocks that showed up across every customer relationship.

Those building blocks became the configurable sections in each loop. Cloud Score trends, open recommendations, case metrics, agenda items. The pattern was consistent enough to standardize but varied enough that rigid templates would fail. That is where the blueprint concept emerged. Structured enough to be useful out of the box, flexible enough that TAMs could tailor each loop to the customer relationship and tell the story they want.

Each TAM's presentation is their own narrative of their customer's AWS environment. They structure it however makes sense for that relationship. We support that.

## Key Decisions

### Blueprints over free-form templates

Instead of a single flexible template, we shipped purpose-built blueprints with content pre-loaded. TAM Check-ins and CSM Success Planning, each with pre-wired data connections. The constraint creates consistency across 30+ customer relationships and makes onboarding faster than a blank canvas would. (More on how we got there below.)

### Live data that freezes on close

Widgets display current data while a loop is open, then snapshot that state when the meeting closes. This preserves historical accuracy. You can see exactly what the environment looked like in February, not what it looks like today. A subtle decision that changes the archival value of every loop significantly.

### Two audiences, one view

TAMs and customers see the same loop. Not a stripped-down customer version and a full internal version. The same view. That forced us to design every element to be legible to both audiences and to think carefully about what's visible, editable, and commentable by each role. It's harder to build, but it's what makes the collaboration real instead of performative.

### Ownership through manual creation

We deliberately scoped out auto-generated loops for the beta. TAMs creating loops themselves meant they had intent and ownership over what they were presenting. Automating it risked loops being ignored because nobody asked for them. Adoption over elegance.

## What I learned

### One hammer, two nails

Midway through the project, I convinced myself we could collapse our three purpose-built blueprints into one flexible, general-purpose one. Easier to maintain, broader reach. On paper, obviously better.

It wasn't. The general-purpose version was minorly helpful to everyone and perfectly helpful to no one. Reviews were polite. People went back to their slide decks.

We pivoted back, then went further: descoped to just TAMs and CSMs, where the efficiency win was highest and the meeting structure was most consistent. Those blueprints got infinitely better the moment we stopped trying to make them work for everyone else. Being mildly useful to a wide audience is worse than being deeply useful to a narrow one.

### Designing for the screenshare

TAMs run customer meetings by screensharing. Loops would replace the content of that screenshare, but not the screenshare itself. So Loops had to be screenshare-safe by default.

The problem: TAMs have internal notes and admin actions the customer shouldn't see, with the customer watching in real time. I first tried a two-view approach, internal and customer-facing, side by side. It was obvious within a week that mistakes would happen. Wrong tab, wrong scroll, stray click.

Next I tried moving all loop management into Atlas, our internal tool, so Mission Control would only ever show the customer view. Cleaner, but TAMs were bouncing between interfaces and we were effectively building the feature twice.

Where I landed was simpler than either: one page that looks the same to everyone, with an actions dropdown on every widget. Same canvas, role-based actions. The screenshare is safe because there's nothing to hide.

### Every widget was its own project

When I sketched the first blueprint, I thought of widgets as containers. Standard rectangles holding standard data. They weren't.

Each widget turned out to be its own design project. Cloud Score is a live data pull with trend logic and inline approve, archive, and snooze. The agenda is a free-form editor with reorderable sections and per-customer toggling. Some widgets create content, some display it, some make the user pick what to show. I thought I was designing one feature with five sections. I was actually designing five features that happened to live in one shell.
